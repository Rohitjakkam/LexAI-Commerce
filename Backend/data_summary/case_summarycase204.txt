## 1. Case Title

* **Case Name:** M/S A2Z Waste Management (Meerut) Pvt. Ltd. Thru. Its Authorized Signatory Mr. Yuvraj Sharma vs. Construction And Design Services, U.P. Jal Nigam, Noida Thru. Director/Project Manager And 2 Others
* **Court:** Allahabad High Court, Lucknow Bench
* **Date of Judgment:** 9th May, 2024
* **Citation:** 2024:AHC-LKO:36014

## 2. Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:** M/S A2Z Waste Management (Meerut) Pvt. Ltd. (Petitioner) filed a petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India seeking a direction for expeditious disposal of an arbitration case (Case No. 831 of 2019) pending in the Commercial Court No. 2, Lucknow. This case was an application under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act filed by the Construction and Design Services, U.P. Jal Nigam (Respondent 1) and U.P. Jal Nigam (Respondent 2) challenging an Arbitration Award dated 29.05.2019 passed in favor of the petitioner.
* **Key Issues:**
    * Whether the Commercial Court should expedite the disposal of the application under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act considering the statutory mandate for expeditious disposal and the objective of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
    * Whether the petitioner's omission to disclose the stay order passed by the Allahabad High Court regarding the proceedings amounted to perjury justifying prosecution under Section 340 Cr.P.C.
    * Whether the conduct of the respondent No. 3, the Municipal Corporation Meerut, in filing a separate application under Section 34 at Meerut, seeking transfer to Lucknow, and then getting the proceedings stayed for two years, was detrimental to the administration of justice and the public exchequer.

## 4. Arguments Presented

* **Plaintiff’s Argument (Petitioner):**
    * The petitioner argued for expeditious disposal of the application under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act due to the statutory mandate and the objective of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
    * The petitioner highlighted the increasing burden of interest on the public exchequer due to the delay in disposal.
    * The petitioner relied on the Supreme Court's concern about delays in disposal of execution cases involving arbitration awards.
    * The petitioner argued that the respondent No. 3's opposition to the petition was without merit, especially considering the award directing the respondents to pay a substantial amount.

* **Defendant’s Argument (Respondent 1 & 2):**
    * The respondents argued that the petitioner sought expeditious disposal only for their application (Case No. 831 of 2019), but not for the application filed by the respondent No. 3 (Case No. 133 of 2023), which should also be decided expeditiously.

* **Defendant’s Argument (Respondent 3):**
    * The respondent No. 3 argued that the petition should be dismissed because the petitioner approached the Court with "unclean hands" by concealing the stay order passed by the Allahabad High Court.
    * The respondent No. 3 filed an application under Section 340 Cr.P.C. for perjury against the petitioner and the deponent of the affidavit.
    * The respondent No. 3 argued that expeditious disposal would lead to violation of law by the Commercial Court in the absence of the arbitrator's record.

## 5. Court’s Analysis and Reasoning

* **Key Findings:**
    * The Court acknowledged the statutory mandate for expeditious disposal of applications under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act.
    * The Court emphasized the objective of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015, to improve the efficiency and reduce delays in commercial cases.
    * The Court found the conduct of the respondent No. 3 in filing a separate application at Meerut, seeking transfer, and then getting the proceedings stayed for two years to be detrimental to the administration of justice and the public exchequer.
    * The Court considered the Supreme Court's concern regarding delays in disposing of execution cases involving arbitration awards and its directions to reduce pendency.

* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The Court interpreted Section 34(6) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, as mandating expeditious disposal of applications under Section 34.
    * The Court interpreted the Commercial Courts Act, 2015, as aiming to improve efficiency and reduce delays in commercial cases.
    * The Court interpreted Section 42 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, as conferring exclusive jurisdiction to the court where the initial application under Part-I of the Act was filed.
    * The Court interpreted Section 340 Cr.P.C. and Section 191 of the IPC in the context of the petitioner's omission to disclose the stay order.

* **Application of Law:**
    * The Court applied the principle of expeditious justice in directing the Commercial Court to dispose of the applications under Section 34 expeditiously.
    * The Court applied the doctrine of "clean hands" in finding the respondent No. 3's conduct detrimental to the administration of justice.
    * The Court applied the strict interpretation of penal laws in dismissing the application under Section 340 Cr.P.C. by finding that the petitioner's omission did not amount to an offence under Section 191 of the IPC.

## 6. Judgment

* **Final Decision:** The petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India was allowed.
* **Relief Granted:** The Court directed the Commercial Court No. 2, Lucknow, to proceed with Arbitration Case No. 831 of 2019 and Arbitration Case No. 133 of 2023 expeditiously without unnecessary adjournments, keeping in view the relevant statutory provisions and the objectives of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
* **Orders:**
    * The Commercial Court was directed to proceed expeditiously without granting unnecessary adjournments.
    * The application under Section 340 Cr.P.C. filed by the respondent No. 3 was dismissed.

## 7. Implications

* **Impact on Law:** The judgment reiterates the importance of expeditious disposal of cases involving arbitration awards and clarifies the application of Section 340 Cr.P.C. in situations where there is an omission to disclose information.
* **Future Relevance:** This case serves as a precedent for similar cases seeking expeditious disposal of arbitration cases and sets a standard for the conduct of parties and the court in such matters.
* **Broader Context:** The judgment highlights the challenges faced by the Indian legal system in dealing with delays in commercial cases and emphasizes the need for efficiency in the administration of justice.

## 8. Summary Points

* The Allahabad High Court directed the Commercial Court to dispose of applications under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act expeditiously considering the statutory mandate and the objective of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
* The Court found the conduct of the respondent No. 3 in delaying the proceedings to be detrimental to the administration of justice and the public exchequer.
* The Court dismissed the application under Section 340 Cr.P.C. filed by the respondent No. 3 for perjury against the petitioner, finding that the petitioner's omission did not amount to an offence under Section 191 of the IPC.
* The judgment emphasizes the importance of expeditious justice in commercial cases and sets a precedent for future similar cases.
