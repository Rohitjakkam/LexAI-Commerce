## Case Title

* **Case Name:** Amar Construction Company vs. Ganesh Buildcon Through Ganesh Babubhai Vandas Patel
* **Court:** Gujarat High Court
* **Date of Judgment:** 19th January 2023
* **Citation:** C/SCA/15744/2021

## Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:** Amar Construction Company (petitioner) was awarded a construction project by the Government of Madhya Pradesh. Ganesh Buildcon (respondent) entered into a sub-let agreement with the petitioner to share the work and profits.  The respondent failed to fulfill his obligations, leading to the petitioner terminating the agreement.  The respondent filed a Special Civil Suit in Patan, claiming dues from the petitioner. The petitioner challenged the jurisdiction of the Patan Court through an application, which was rejected. 
* **Key Issues:**
    *  Whether the Patan Court had jurisdiction to hear the respondent's suit.
    *  Whether the petitioner's application under Order 7, Rule 10 of the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC) should have been granted. 

## Legal Principles Involved

* **Relevant Statutes and Provisions:**
    * **Section 20 of the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC):**  Determines the jurisdiction of courts based on the defendant's residence, place of business, or where the cause of action arises.
    * **Section 12A of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015:**  Provides for pre-institution mediation in commercial disputes.
    * **Section 13 of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015:**  Outlines the process for appealing judgments of Commercial Courts.
    * **Order 7, Rule 10 of the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC):** Allows for a plaint to be returned to the plaintiff if it is found to be defective. 
* **Precedents Cited:**
    * **A.B.C. Laminart Pvt. Ltd. and another v. A.P. Agencies, Salem, AIR 1989 SC 1239:**  Deals with the issue of jurisdiction based on RTGS transactions.
    * **Shridhar Vyapaar Pvt. Ltd. v. Gammon India Ltd., 2016 Calcutta High Court:**  Concerns the determination of jurisdiction in commercial disputes.
    * **N.N. Global Mercantile (P) Ltd. v. Indo Unique Flame Ltd., (2021) 4 SCC 379:**  Addresses the maintainability of writ petitions when statutory remedies exist. 
* **Legal Doctrines:**
    * **The Debtor Must Seek the Creditor:**  A legal principle stating that the debtor must go to the creditor's jurisdiction to resolve a dispute.

## Arguments Presented

* **Petitioner’s Argument:**
    * The sub-let agreement was executed in Mehsana.
    * The work was done in Jabalpur, Madhya Pradesh.
    * The respondent did not establish any cause of action arising in Patan. 
    * The trial Court erred in relying on the respondent's letterhead, which showed the petitioner's office in Patan, as it was an outdated document.
    * The petitioner's Mehsana address was clearly stated in their written statement and other documents.
    * The respondent did not mention the Patan office in their reply to the petitioner's application.
    * The RTGS transaction was initiated by the petitioner from their Mehsana bank account.
    * The petitioner is in the process of filing a suit against the respondent in the competent court at Mehsana.

* **Respondent’s Argument:**
    * The suit is a commercial dispute and is pending in the Commercial Court, Patan.
    * The respondent had entered an appearance and filed a reply.
    * The petitioner's letter dated 8.11.2019, annexed as Annexure E to the petition, clearly shows the petitioner's head office in Patan.
    * The letterhead, dated 8.11.2019, confirms that the petitioner operated under Patan jurisdiction.
    * The respondent's bank account in Patan was used for receiving part of the payments, giving the Patan Court jurisdiction based on the "Debtor Must Seek the Creditor" doctrine. 
    * The petitioner's argument regarding the outdated letterhead was misleading and intended to deceive the court.

## Court’s Analysis and Reasoning

* **Key Findings:** 
    * The respondent had filed the suit in the Commercial Court, Patan, after both parties underwent pre-institution mediation.
    * The suit involved a value exceeding Rs. 3 Lakh, falling under the jurisdiction of the Commercial Court, Patan. 
* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The court referred to the notifications conferring the jurisdiction of Commercial Courts to Senior Civil Judges with experience in commercial disputes. 
    * The court clarified that appeals against Commercial Court judgments are governed by Section 13 of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015. 
    * The court applied the precedent in N.N. Global Mercantile (P) Ltd. v. Indo Unique Flame Ltd., (2021) 4 SCC 379, stating that writ petitions are not maintainable when statutory remedies are available. 
* **Application of Law:**
    * The court concluded that the petitioner's application challenging the jurisdiction of the Commercial Court, Patan, was not maintainable as it was filed under Article 227 of the Constitution.
    * The court directed the petitioner to pursue the statutory remedy of filing an appeal under Section 13 of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.

## Judgment

* **Final Decision:**
    * The petitioner's petition under Article 227 of the Constitution was dismissed.
* **Relief Granted:**
    *  None.
* **Orders:**
    * The petitioner was relegated to pursue the statutory remedy of filing an appeal under Section 13 of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015. 
    * The time period during which the petition was pending before the High Court was to be deducted while considering the limitation period for any future appeal.

## Implications

* **Impact on Law:**
    * The judgment reinforces the need to exhaust statutory remedies available under the Commercial Courts Act, 2015, before resorting to writ petitions.
* **Future Relevance:**
    * This case provides a guide for parties involved in commercial disputes in understanding the scope of jurisdiction and the appropriate remedies available under the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
* **Broader Context:**
    * The judgment emphasizes the importance of clearly establishing the jurisdiction of courts in commercial disputes.

## Summary Points

* The petitioner's challenge to the jurisdiction of the Commercial Court, Patan, was rejected. 
* The petitioner was advised to file an appeal under Section 13 of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
* The judgment underscores the need to utilize statutory remedies within the Commercial Courts Act before approaching the High Court.
