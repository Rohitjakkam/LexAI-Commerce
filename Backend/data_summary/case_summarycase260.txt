## Case Title

* **Case Name:** Tapesh Arora vs. Mukesh Chand 
* **Court:** Delhi High Court
* **Date of Judgment:** 16th November 2023
* **Citation:**  CM(M) 1806/2023 (Delhi High Court)

## Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:** This case involves a dispute over a property, where Mukesh Chand (Respondent) filed a civil suit seeking possession of the property (A-79, Group Industrial Area, Wazirpur, Delhi) from Tapesh Arora (Petitioner). 
* **Key Issues:**
    * **Jurisdiction of the Commercial Court:** The Petitioner challenged the Commercial Court's jurisdiction to try the suit, arguing that the pre-institution mediation requirement under Section 12-A of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 wasn't fulfilled.
    * **Maintainability of Appeal:** The Petitioner argued that an appeal against the decree of possession wasn't available under the Commercial Courts Act, 2015, citing the proviso to Section 13(1A).
    * **Valuation of the Suit:** The Petitioner argued that the relief of possession was incorrectly valued in the plaint, which impacted the Commercial Court's jurisdiction.

## Legal Principles Involved

* **Relevant Statutes and Provisions:**
    * **Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC):**  
        * Order XII Rule 6 (application for judgment)
        * Section 2(2) (definition of decree)
        * Section 96 (appeal against decrees)
        * Order XLI (procedure for appeals)
        * Order VIII Rule 3A (5) (pleading requirements in Commercial Courts)
        * Order XXXIX Rule 1 and 2 (interim relief)
    * **Commercial Courts Act, 2015:**
        * Section 12 (3) (election of forum)
        * Section 13 (1A) (appeals against judgments of District Judge level Commercial Courts)
        * Section 12-A (pre-institution mediation requirement)
    * **Constitution of India:**
        * Article 227 (supervisory jurisdiction of High Courts)
    * **Court Fees Act, 1870:**
        * Section 7 (xi) (valuation of landlord-tenant suits)
* **Precedents Cited:**
    * **Kandla Export Corporation and Anr. v. OCI Corporation and Anr., (2018) 14 SCC 715:** Supreme Court decision on the scope of Section 13 of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
    * **Kandla Container Terminal Pvt. Ltd. v. Doosan Heavy Industries and Construction Company Ltd., 2018 SCC OnLine Guj 4051:** Gujarat High Court decision interpreting Section 13 of the Act.
    * **HPL (India) limited & Ors. v. QRD Enterprises and Anr., 2017 SCC Online Del 6955:** Delhi High Court decision on the meaning of "judgment" in Section 13(1) of the Act.
    * **Virudhunagar Hindu Nadargal Dharma Paribalana Sabai and Ors. v. Tuticorin Educational Society and Ors., (2019) 9 SCC 538:** Supreme Court decision on the High Court's role when a statutory remedy exists in the CPC.
    * **Patil Automation Pvt. Ltd. & Ors. v. Rakheja Engineers Pvt. Ltd., (2022) 10 SCC 1:** Supreme Court decision on the mandatory nature of Section 12-A of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
    * **Chandra Kishore Chaurasia v. R A Perfumery Works Pvt. Ltd., 2022 SCC OnLine Del 3529:** Delhi High Court decision on the applicability of Section 12-A when urgent interim relief is sought.
* **Legal Doctrines:**
    * **Jurisdiction:** The power of a court to hear and determine a particular case.
    * **Res Judicata:** A doctrine preventing the relitigation of issues already decided by a court.
    * **Statutory Interpretation:** The process of interpreting and applying statutory provisions.

## Arguments Presented

* **Petitioner's Arguments:**
    * The Commercial Court lacked jurisdiction as the Respondent hadn't undergone pre-institution mediation under Section 12-A of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
    * An appeal against the decree of possession was not available under the Commercial Courts Act, 2015.
    * The relief of possession was incorrectly valued in the plaint, affecting the Commercial Court's jurisdiction.
* **Respondent's Arguments:**
    * The arguments of the Respondent are not mentioned in detail in the order, but the Court states that it intends to hear the Respondent on the issues raised by the Petitioner. 

## Courtâ€™s Analysis and Reasoning

* **Key Findings:**
    * The Court found that the Petitioner had a statutory remedy of appeal available under Section 96 read with Order XLI of the CPC against the decree of possession.
    * The Court dismissed the Petitioner's argument that the proviso to Section 13(1A) of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 barred appeal.
    * The Court found that the Petitioner hadn't raised a specific plea disputing the valuation of the relief in the written statement, as required under Order VIII Rule 3A (5) of the CPC.
    * The Court found that the Respondent's application for interim relief under Order XXXIX Rule 1 and 2 of the CPC, filed along with the plaint, exempted the Respondent from the pre-institution mediation requirement under Section 12-A of the Act. 
    * The Court upheld the valuation of the relief of possession based on the rent of the property, as per Section 7 (xi) of the Court Fees Act, 1870.
* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The Court interpreted Section 13(1A) of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 to include appeals against decrees of possession, relying on the Supreme Court's decision in Kandla Export Corporation.
    * The Court interpreted Order VIII Rule 3A (5) of the CPC to require a specific plea regarding valuation in the written statement.
    * The Court interpreted Section 12-A of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 to exempt suits seeking urgent interim relief from the pre-institution mediation requirement.
* **Application of Law:**
    * The Court applied the relevant statutory provisions and precedent to conclude that the Petitioner had a remedy of appeal available under the CPC.
    * The Court applied the relevant provisions to determine that the Respondent was exempt from pre-institution mediation due to the application for urgent interim relief.
    * The Court applied Section 7 (xi) of the Court Fees Act, 1870 to validate the valuation of the relief of possession.

## Judgment

* **Final Decision:** The High Court dismissed the Petitioner's application seeking a stay of the decree of possession.
* **Relief Granted:** No relief was granted to the Petitioner.
* **Orders:**
    * The Petitioner was directed to re-serve a copy of the petition to the Respondent through speed post, courier, and email.
    * The proceedings before the Commercial Court were to continue, with no stay being sought on the ground of the pending petition. 
    * The matter was listed before the Joint Registrar (Judicial) on 05.01.2024 and before the Court on 24.01.2024.

## Implications

* **Impact on Law:** This judgment clarifies the applicability of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 in cases involving decrees of possession and urgent interim relief.
* **Future Relevance:** This case provides guidance on the procedural requirements for challenging decrees of possession in Commercial Courts and the applicability of the pre-institution mediation requirement under the Act.
* **Broader Context:** This case highlights the importance of following statutory remedies and raising specific pleas in court proceedings.

## Summary Points

* The Delhi High Court held that the Petitioner had a statutory remedy of appeal against the decree of possession under the CPC.
* The Court dismissed the Petitioner's arguments that an appeal was not available under the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 and that the Respondent hadn't fulfilled the pre-institution mediation requirement.
* The Court found that the Respondent's application for urgent interim relief exempted him from the pre-institution mediation requirement.
* The Court upheld the valuation of the relief of possession based on the rent of the property.
