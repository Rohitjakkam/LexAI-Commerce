## 1. Case Title

* **Case Name:** Mr. K.N. Mahesh Prasad vs Optimus Drugs Private Limited And 2 Others
* **Court:** Telangana High Court
* **Date of Judgment:** 12th June 2023
* **Citation:**  Commercial Court Appeal No.20 of 2023

## 2. Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:**  Mr. K.N. Mahesh Prasad (Plaintiff) sued Optimus Drugs Private Limited (Defendant 1) and others (Defendants 2 & 3), alleging non-compliance with a Consultancy and Shareholders' Agreement (Ex.P.1-Agreement) dated 03.11.2018.  The agreement outlined payment obligations, transfer of shares, and call options for the plaintiff in exchange for his consultancy services. 
* **Key Issues:**
    * Whether the plaintiff had fulfilled his obligations under the agreement, particularly regarding providing consultancy services.
    * Whether the defendants had breached the agreement by not paying monthly compensation, transferring sale shares, and transferring consultancy stock (call option shares).
    * Whether the plaintiff had met the necessary conditions to exercise his call option rights under the agreement.
    * Whether the trial court erred in refusing to grant a temporary injunction to restrain the defendants from altering their shareholding pattern.

## 3. Legal Principles Involved

* **Relevant Statutes and Provisions:**
    * Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC):
        * Order XXXIX Rules 1 and 2 (Temporary Injunctions)
        * Section 151 (Inherent Powers of the Court)
    * Specific Relief Act, 1872:
        * Section 16(c) (Readiness and Willingness to Perform)
    * Sale of Goods Act, 1930:
        * Section 4 & 2(10) (Contract of Sale, Payment as Consideration)
        * Section 9 (Determination of Price)
    * Contract Act, 1872:
        * Section 29 (Agreements Void for Uncertainty)

* **Precedents Cited:**
    * M.S. Madhusoodhanan Vs Kerala Kaumudi (P) Ltd (2004) 9 SCC 204
    * J.B.Builders Vs A.Ramadas Rao (2011) 1 SCC 429

* **Legal Doctrines:**
    * **Prima facie case:** Whether the plaintiff has presented sufficient evidence to suggest a likely success in the main suit.
    * **Balance of convenience:** Whether the potential harm to the plaintiff outweighs any potential harm to the defendants if an injunction is granted.
    * **Irreparable injury/prejudice:** Whether the harm to the plaintiff cannot be adequately compensated by monetary damages.
    * **Readiness and willingness:** Whether the plaintiff has demonstrated readiness and willingness to fulfill their part of the agreement before seeking specific performance.

## 4. Arguments Presented

* **Plaintiff’s Argument:**
    * The plaintiff had fulfilled his obligations by providing consultancy services, including procuring partnerships, obtaining loans, setting up workspaces, and identifying business opportunities.
    * The defendants failed to pay monthly compensation and refused to transfer sale shares and consultancy stock despite achieving the agreed-upon EBITDA milestones.
    * The defendants were unlawfully transferring shares to third parties in violation of the agreement.
    * The plaintiff issued legal notices demanding compliance and exercised his call option rights.
    * The defendants failed to respond to the notices and continued to breach the agreement.
    * The plaintiff suffered irreparable loss due to the defendants' actions.

* **Defendant’s Argument:**
    * The plaintiff did not provide adequate consultancy services and failed to discharge his responsibilities.
    * The plaintiff had not claimed monthly compensation for a long time, suggesting he was not performing services.
    * The transfer of sale shares was contingent upon the plaintiff's services, which he failed to render.
    * The plaintiff's call option notice did not meet the conditions outlined in the agreement, particularly regarding specifying the number of shares, date of purchase, and price.
    * The plaintiff's claim for transfer of shares was barred by delay and laches.

## 5. Court’s Analysis and Reasoning

* **Key Findings:**
    * The court acknowledged that the plaintiff's claim for monthly compensation, transfer of sale shares, and transfer of call option shares were disputed issues requiring further examination during the trial.
    * The court found that the plaintiff's call option notice did not comply with the terms of the agreement, failing to specify the necessary details as required by Clause 4(IV).
    * The court considered the relevant precedents on readiness and willingness,  emphasizing the plaintiff's obligation to demonstrate these before seeking specific performance.
    * The court highlighted the discretionary nature of injunctions under Order XXXIX Rules 1 and 2 of CPC, requiring satisfaction of the three parameters of prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injury.

* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The court carefully analyzed the clauses of the agreement, particularly those related to payment obligations, share transfer, and call option rights.
    * The court interpreted the agreement to require the plaintiff to fulfill specific conditions before exercising his call option rights, including providing a detailed notice.
    * The court cited relevant precedents on contract law, specific performance, and injunctions, applying the principles to the specific facts of the case.

* **Application of Law:**
    * The court applied the three parameters of prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injury to assess the plaintiff's claim for a temporary injunction.
    * Based on the evidence presented, the court concluded that the plaintiff had not established a strong enough prima facie case to warrant the grant of an injunction.
    * The court considered the defendants' arguments regarding the plaintiff's non-performance and the absence of a valid call option notice, ultimately concluding that the balance of convenience did not favor the plaintiff.

## 6. Judgment

* **Final Decision:** The appeal was dismissed.
* **Relief Granted:** No relief was granted.
* **Orders:** The trial court's order refusing to grant a temporary injunction was upheld. The court directed that any future sale transactions by the defendants would be subject to the outcome of the main suit (COS No.32 of 2022).

## 7. Implications

* **Impact on Law:** The judgment clarifies the requirements for exercising call option rights under contracts, emphasizing the importance of fulfilling specific conditions outlined in the agreement. It also reiterates the discretionary nature of injunctions and the need for the plaintiff to establish a strong case to warrant their grant.
* **Future Relevance:** This case serves as a precedent for future disputes involving contractual obligations, specifically regarding share transfers and call option rights. It highlights the critical role of providing detailed notices and demonstrating readiness and willingness to perform contractual obligations.
* **Broader Context:** The case underscores the importance of clearly defined terms and conditions in commercial agreements to avoid future disputes and ensure clarity in the transfer of shares and exercise of call option rights. 

## 8. Summary Points

* The court dismissed the plaintiff's appeal seeking a temporary injunction to restrain the defendants from altering their shareholding pattern.
* The court found that the plaintiff had not fulfilled the necessary conditions to exercise his call option rights under the agreement, specifically failing to provide a detailed notice as required.
* The court emphasized the importance of demonstrating readiness and willingness to perform contractual obligations before seeking specific performance.
* The judgment highlights the discretionary nature of injunctions and the need for the plaintiff to establish a strong prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injury.
