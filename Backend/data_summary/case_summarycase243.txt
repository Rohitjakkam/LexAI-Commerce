## Case Title

* **Case Name:** Gujarat Composite Limited vs Apurva Vinimay Private Limited
* **Court:** Gujarat High Court
* **Date of Judgment:** 23rd April 2018
* **Citation:** C/FA/588/2018, C/FA/587/2018

## Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:** Gujarat Composite Limited (GCL), the original defendant, entered into license agreements with Apurva Vinimay Private Limited (AVPL) and its sister concern, granting AVPL the right to operate GCL's manufacturing units.  GCL received financial assistance from AVPL through a supplementary agreement, allowing AVPL to create a mortgage on the manufacturing units.  A tripartite agreement was later executed involving GCL, AVPL, and the Bank of Baroda, where GCL mortgaged the units to the bank for a corporate loan obtained by AVPL to help GCL.  The dispute arose when GCL refused to extend the license agreement and AVPL continued to operate the units.  GCL issued a notice to AVPL demanding possession and payment, leading to a series of attempts to resolve the dispute before GCL invoked the arbitration clause in the license agreement.  AVPL filed a suit against GCL, the Bank of Baroda, and other parties who purchased property from GCL, seeking various reliefs.
* **Key Issues:** Whether the dispute in AVPL's suit should be referred to arbitration under the license agreement considering the presence of non-signatory parties and multiple agreements.

## Legal Principles Involved

* **Relevant Statutes and Provisions:** 
    * Section 8 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (Arbitration Act, 1996): Deals with the power of a judicial authority to refer parties to arbitration where an arbitration agreement exists.
    * Section 24 of the Arbitration Act, 1940: Provided for the referral of specific matters between parties to a suit to arbitration, with the suit continuing for other parties not involved in the arbitration.
    * Section 89 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908: Provides for the settlement of disputes through alternate dispute resolution methods.
    * Order VI, Rule 4 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908: Requires specific particulars of alleged fraudulent acts to be pleaded.

    * **Severability of Contracts:** The principle that different parts of a contract can be separated and applied independently.
    * **Fraud:** A deliberate act of deception intended to gain an unfair advantage.
    * **Estoppel:** The legal principle that prevents a person from contradicting their prior statements or actions.

## Arguments Presented

* **Plaintiff’s Argument (AVPL):**
    * The suit is primarily a mortgage suit, seeking to prevent the Bank of Baroda from releasing the mortgage without AVPL's consent.
    * The dispute involves multiple agreements (license, supplementary, tripartite) with varying provisions, including the tripartite agreement which does not contain an arbitration clause.
    * The inclusion of non-signatory parties (Bank of Baroda, third-party purchasers) makes the entire matter non-arbitrable.
    * The presence of alleged fraudulent acts by GCL in selling the mortgaged property to third parties necessitates judicial determination and cannot be resolved through arbitration.
    * The complex legal and factual issues involved in the suit warrant adjudication by the court.
* **Defendant’s Argument (GCL):**
    * The arbitration clause in the license agreement covers the entire dispute, including AVPL's claims for recovery and possession.
    * AVPL's pleadings are vague and ambiguous, based primarily on the license agreement.
    * The inclusion of third parties (purchasers) does not render the matter non-arbitrable, as they have expressed willingness to join arbitration.
    * The Bank of Baroda's involvement is minimal, as its dues have already been paid, and GCL is prepared to deposit the remaining amount with the court.
    * Fraud simplicitor cannot be a ground to reject an application under Section 8 of the Arbitration Act, 1996.
    * AVPL is estopped from seeking relief through any other forum, as it previously agreed to resolve disputes through the mechanism outlined in the license agreement.
    * The court should follow the principle of "special law" overriding "general law" and prioritize arbitration.

## Court’s Analysis and Reasoning

* **Key Findings:**
    * The suit involved multiple causes of action and multiple defendants, some of whom were parties to the arbitration agreement and others who were not.
    * The tripartite agreement did not contain an arbitration clause and constituted an independent agreement concerning the mortgage.
    * The suit sought reliefs against both parties to the arbitration agreement (GCL) and non-parties (Bank of Baroda, third-party purchasers).
    * The court could not bifurcate the dispute between the parties to the arbitration agreement and the non-parties.
    * The court could not rely on the precedents cited by GCL, as those cases involved different factual scenarios and legal issues.
* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The court interpreted the word "a matter" in Section 8 of the Arbitration Act, 1996 to mean the entire subject matter of the suit.
    * The court found no provision in the Act for bifurcation of the suit into arbitrable and non-arbitrable parts.
    * The court upheld the principle that fraud simplicitor is not a valid ground to refuse arbitration.
* **Application of Law:**
    * The court applied the law laid down in the Supreme Court case of Sukanya Holdings (P) Ltd. and other precedents to determine that the dispute was not fully covered by the arbitration agreement.
    * The court found that the presence of non-parties and multiple causes of action prevented the dispute from being referred to arbitration under Section 8 of the Arbitration Act, 1996.

## Judgment

* **Final Decision:** The First Appeals were dismissed. 
* **Relief Granted:** No specific relief was granted as the appeals were dismissed. 
* **Orders:** The court directed that there would be no order as to costs.

## Implications

* **Impact on Law:** The judgment reaffirms the principle that Section 8 of the Arbitration Act, 1996 applies only when the entire subject matter of the suit falls under the scope of the arbitration agreement. It further clarifies that disputes cannot be bifurcated into arbitrable and non-arbitrable parts.
* **Future Relevance:** This case serves as a guide for courts in determining the arbitrability of disputes involving multiple parties and agreements. It highlights the need for careful consideration of the scope of the arbitration agreement and the presence of non-signatory parties.
* **Broader Context:** The case reflects the importance of clear and comprehensive arbitration agreements in complex commercial transactions. It underscores the need for parties to carefully consider the potential involvement of non-parties and the risks of non-arbitrable disputes.

## Summary Points

* The Gujarat High Court dismissed the appeals because the dispute in the suit involved multiple parties and agreements, some of which did not contain an arbitration clause.
* The court upheld the principle that Section 8 of the Arbitration Act, 1996 applies only when the entire subject matter of the suit is subject to the arbitration agreement.
* The court found no provision for bifurcation of the dispute into arbitrable and non-arbitrable parts.
