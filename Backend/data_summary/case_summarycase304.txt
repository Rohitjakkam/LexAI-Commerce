## 1. Case Title

* **Case Name:** Aakash Engineers And Contractors vs. Presiding Officer Commercial Court Gauytam Buddh Nagar And Another
* **Court:** Allahabad High Court
* **Date of Judgment:** 14 August 2020
* **Citation:**  Not provided in the text. 

## 2. Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:** The petitioner, Aakash Engineers and Contractors, filed a petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India seeking a direction to the Presiding Officer of the Commercial Court in Gautam Buddh Nagar to expedite the disposal of an arbitration case filed under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (hereinafter referred to as "the Act of 1996"). The arbitration case, registered as Arbitration Case No. 110 of 2018, involved the petitioner as the respondent and Gaursons Promoters Pvt. Ltd. as the applicant, and had been pending for over a year. 
* **Key Issues:** The main legal questions involved were: 
    * Whether the courts have a duty to ensure expeditious disposal of arbitration cases under Section 34 of the Act of 1996, despite the provisions being considered directory.
    * What constitutes "substantial compliance" with the time limits prescribed in Section 34(5) and (6) of the Act of 1996 for disposing of arbitration applications.

## 3. Legal Principles Involved

* **Relevant Statutes and Provisions:**
    * Article 227 of the Constitution of India:  Permits the High Court to exercise supervisory jurisdiction over subordinate courts. 
    * The Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (Act of 1996):  Provides a legal framework for arbitration in India. 
    * Section 34 of the Act of 1996: Deals with applications for setting aside arbitral awards.
    * Section 34(5) and (6) of the Act of 1996: Stipulates that applications under Section 34 should be filed with a prior notice to the other party and disposed of expeditiously, within a period of one year from the date the notice is served. 
* **Precedents Cited:**  Numerous judgments from the Supreme Court and the Allahabad High Court were cited, including:
    * M/s Shiv Cotex Versus Tirgum Auto Plast P. Ltd. and others, 2011 (89) ALR 232
    * Noor Mohammed Vs. Jethanand and another, (2013) 5 SCC 202
    * Gayathri Vs. M.Girish, (2016) 14 SCC 142
    * State of Bihar and others Vs. Bihar Rajya Bhumi Vikas Bank Samiti, (2018) 9 SCC 472
    * Tribhuwan Prasad Vs. Uttar Pradesh Sarkar and others, 2018 (9) ADJ 466
* **Legal Doctrines:** The judgment delved into various legal doctrines, including: 
    * The principle of "expeditious justice" as a fundamental tenet of the Indian judicial system.
    * The concept of "directory provisions" in statutes, wherein strict adherence is not mandatory but substantial compliance is expected.
    * The application of the "doctrine of substantial compliance" in interpreting time limits prescribed in statutes.
    * The court's power to exercise supervisory jurisdiction under Article 227 of the Constitution of India to ensure the efficient functioning of subordinate courts.

## 4. Arguments Presented

* **Plaintiff’s Argument:**  The petitioner argued that the arbitration case had been pending for an unreasonably long time, resulting in a denial of justice. The petitioner emphasized the importance of timely disposal of arbitration cases under the Act of 1996 to uphold the principle of expeditious justice and maintain public confidence in the judicial system. 
* **Defendant’s Argument:** The respondent's counsel, Sri Nikhil Agrawal, did not object to the matter being disposed of within a stipulated period prescribed by the court. 

## 5. Court’s Analysis and Reasoning

* **Key Findings:** The court found that: 
    * The prolonged pendency of the arbitration case was unacceptable and a violation of the legislative mandate contained in Section 34(5) and (6) of the Act of 1996.
    * While the provisions regarding time limits in Section 34(5) and (6) were considered directory, the court emphasized the importance of substantial compliance with these timeframes to avoid indefinite delays.
    * The court reiterated the duty of all stakeholders in the judicial system, including judges, lawyers, and litigants, to ensure expeditious disposal of cases.
    * The court also recognized the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on court operations but stressed that the rule of law must prevail even in exceptional circumstances. 
* **Interpretation of Law:** The court interpreted Section 34(5) and (6) of the Act of 1996 as directory in nature, which meant that strict adherence to the timeframes was not mandatory, but substantial compliance was essential. The court also drew upon various previous judgments from the Supreme Court and other High Courts to elucidate the concept of substantial compliance and its implications.
* **Application of Law:** The court applied the legal principles discussed above to the specific facts of the case. It observed that the order-sheet of the Commercial Court revealed a lack of diligent efforts to conclude the proceedings within a reasonable time frame. The court also highlighted instances of unnecessary adjournments and delays attributed to both parties and the court itself. 

## 6. Judgment

* **Final Decision:**  The court directed the Presiding Officer of the Commercial Court to expeditiously dispose of the arbitration case within a period of six months from the date of receipt of a certified copy of the order.
* **Relief Granted:** The petitioner's plea for expeditious disposal of the arbitration case was granted. 
* **Orders:**  The court issued a series of specific orders to ensure the timely disposal of the arbitration case, including:
    * A six-month time limit for concluding the proceedings.
    * A prohibition on granting unnecessary adjournments.
    * Imposition of costs on parties seeking adjournments.
    * Instructions to proceed with the case even in the absence of counsel, if necessary.
    * Provisions for handling strike calls by advocates.
    * Directions to fix at least two hearing dates every week and, if required, proceed on a day-to-day basis.
    * Authority to transfer the case to another court if the Presiding Officer is unavailable. 
    * A provision for adjusting the timeline in the event of court closures due to COVID-19.

## 7. Implications

* **Impact on Law:** The judgment reiterates the importance of expeditious disposal of arbitration cases under the Act of 1996, despite the directory nature of the provisions in Section 34(5) and (6). It emphasizes the need for courts to actively ensure substantial compliance with these timeframes.
* **Future Relevance:** This case serves as a precedent for future cases involving delays in arbitration proceedings and the court's power to exercise supervisory jurisdiction under Article 227 to ensure the timely delivery of justice. 
* **Broader Context:**  The judgment underscores the critical role of all stakeholders in the judicial system in fostering a culture of efficiency and timely resolution of disputes, particularly in the context of arbitration proceedings.  It also emphasizes the importance of upholding the rule of law even during times of crisis, like the COVID-19 pandemic. 

## 8. Summary Points

* The Allahabad High Court issued a judgment mandating the expeditious disposal of an arbitration case pending for over a year. 
* The court, while acknowledging the directory nature of time limits in Section 34(5) and (6) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, emphasized the importance of substantial compliance with these provisions.
* The judgment underscores the court's duty to ensure expeditious justice and maintain public confidence in the judicial system.
* The court imposed a six-month time limit for concluding the arbitration case, along with a series of directives to address delays and ensure efficient proceedings.
