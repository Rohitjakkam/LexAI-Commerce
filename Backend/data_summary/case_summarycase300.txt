## 1. Case Title

* **Case Name:** Aakash Engineers Contractors vs. Presiding Officer Commercial Court And Another
* **Court:** Allahabad High Court
* **Date of Judgment:** 14th August 2020
* **Citation:** Not provided in the text.

## 2. Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:** The petitioner, Aakash Engineers Contractors, filed a petition under Article 227 of the Constitution of India seeking directions from the Allahabad High Court to the Presiding Officer, Commercial Court, Gautam Budh Nagar to expeditiously decide Arbitration Case No. 109 of 2018 filed under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (Gaursons Hi-Tech Infrastructures Pvt. Ltd. Vs. Aakash Engineers and Contractors). The case had been pending for over a year and four months without any significant progress.
* **Key Issues:**
    * Whether the Presiding Officer, Commercial Court, Gautam Budh Nagar was delaying the disposal of the Arbitration Case No. 109 of 2018.
    * Whether the provisions of Section 34(5) and (6) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, were mandatory or directory.
    * What are the consequences of not adhering to the timeframes set out in the statute for deciding applications under Section 34.

## 4. Arguments Presented

* **Petitioner’s Argument:**
    * The petitioner argued that the Arbitration Case No. 109 of 2018 was pending for an unreasonable period of time, causing significant prejudice to them.
    * They argued that the Presiding Officer, Commercial Court, Gautam Budh Nagar was not making sincere efforts to dispose of the case expeditiously.
    * They cited various precedents to support their argument that courts have a duty to ensure speedy disposal of cases, especially those under the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996.
* **Respondent’s Argument:**
    * The respondent, through their counsel, did not object to the case being decided within a stipulated timeframe. 
    * However, the court chose to delve deeper into the issue of delay and the interpretation of Section 34(5) and (6) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, for the benefit of all parties involved and for guidance of the trial court.

## 5. Court’s Analysis and Reasoning

* **Key Findings:**
    * The court noted that the case had been pending for over a year and four months without any significant progress.
    * The court reviewed the order-sheet of the trial court, which revealed a pattern of repeated adjournments and delays caused by various factors, including the absence of the Presiding Officer, adjournments sought by counsels, and the lack of urgency in the proceedings.
    * The court emphasized the importance of timely justice delivery and the need to address the issue of delays in the judicial system.
* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The court analyzed the provisions of Section 34(5) and (6) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, and reviewed several previous Supreme Court judgments on the matter.
    * The court ultimately held that Section 34(5) and (6) of the Act were directory in nature, meaning that they were not mandatory but rather guidelines for the courts to adhere to. 
    * The court cited the reasoning from the Supreme Court case *State of Bihar and others Vs. Bihar Rajya Bhumi Vikas Bank Samiti* (2018) 9 SCC 472, which stated that the lack of specific consequences for non-compliance within the timeframe indicated that the provisions were not mandatory.
* **Application of Law:**
    * The court applied the doctrine of substantial compliance to the directory provisions of Section 34(5) and (6).
    * The court acknowledged that while strict adherence to the timeframes set out in the statute was not mandatory, substantial efforts to dispose of the case within a reasonable time were required.
    * The court held that the Presiding Officer, Commercial Court, Gautam Budh Nagar had not demonstrated sufficient effort to dispose of the case within a reasonable timeframe. 
    * The court further emphasized that the order-sheet of the court was the most reliable evidence of the court's efforts and diligence in disposing of the case.

## 6. Judgment

* **Final Decision:** The petition was allowed.
* **Relief Granted:** The court directed the Presiding Officer, Commercial Court, Gautam Budh Nagar to finally dispose of the Arbitration Case No. 109 of 2018 within a period of six months from the date of receipt of a certified copy of the order.
* **Orders:**
    * The court directed the Presiding Officer to fix at least two dates every week for the case and proceed on a day-to-day basis if necessary, even in the absence of counsels, to ensure the strict adherence to the six-month timeframe.
    * The court prohibited the granting of unnecessary adjournments and mandated the imposition of costs on parties seeking unnecessary adjournments.
    * The court further directed the Presiding Officer to take out appropriate legal proceedings against erring counsels for flouting directions of the Supreme Court regarding strike calls.
    * The order was held in abeyance in case the court was shut down due to any COVID-19 pandemic related emergency, but it was to become operative immediately upon reopening, with the timeline adjusted accordingly.

## 7. Implications

* **Impact on Law:**
    * This judgment reinforces the courts' commitment to timely justice delivery and reiterates the responsibility of courts to address delays in the judicial system.
    * The judgment clarifies that while Section 34(5) and (6) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, are directory in nature, courts are still expected to make substantial efforts to dispose of cases within a reasonable time.
    * The court's direction to proceed on a day-to-day basis if necessary could set a precedent for other cases with similar issues of delay.
* **Future Relevance:**
    * This case sets a precedent for judicial review under Article 227 in cases involving delays in the disposal of arbitration proceedings.
    * It also provides guidance to trial courts regarding the interpretation and implementation of Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996.
* **Broader Context:**
    * This judgment highlights the ongoing challenge of delays in the Indian judicial system and underscores the need for judicial reforms to address this issue.
    * It also emphasizes the courts' role in ensuring that the right to speedy justice, as enshrined in the Constitution, is upheld.

## 8. Summary Points

* The Allahabad High Court directed the Presiding Officer, Commercial Court, Gautam Budh Nagar, to dispose of an arbitration case within six months.
* The court held that the provisions of Section 34(5) and (6) of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, are directory, meaning that they are not mandatory but rather guidelines for the courts to adhere to.
* The court emphasized the importance of timely justice delivery and the need to address the issue of delays in the judicial system.
* The judgment sets a precedent for judicial review under Article 227 in cases involving delays in the disposal of arbitration proceedings.
