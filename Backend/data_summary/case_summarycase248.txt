## Case Summary: J.P. Realties Pvt. Ltd. vs. Mahesh S/O Chandrabhan Kingrani

**1. Case Title**

* **Case Name:** J.P. Realties Pvt. Ltd. & J.P. Housing Pvt. Ltd. vs. Mahesh S/O Chandrabhan Kingrani & Ors. 
* **Court:** Bombay High Court, Nagpur Bench
* **Date of Judgment:** 19th March 2024
* **Citation:** 2024:BHC-NAG:3381

**2. Background and Context**

* **Brief Overview:** This case involved two separate suits (Special Civil Suit Nos. 248 of 2023 and 1235/2022) filed by Mahesh and Rajesh Kingrani (non-applicants 1 & 2) against J.P. Realties Pvt. Ltd. and J.P. Housing Pvt. Ltd. (applicants) seeking specific performance of agreements for the sale of land. The land was intended for development into a commercial/residential project. 
* **Key Issues:** The main issue was whether the dispute was a "commercial dispute" as defined under the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 (Act of 2015). This determination would decide the court's jurisdiction to handle the matter.

**3. Legal Principles Involved**

* **Relevant Statutes and Provisions:**
    * **Code of Civil Procedure (CPC), Section 115:** This section grants the High Court revisional jurisdiction to revise orders passed by subordinate courts.
    * **Commercial Courts Act, 2015 (Act of 2015):** This Act establishes commercial courts to handle commercial disputes efficiently.
    * **CPC, Order VII Rule 11:** This rule allows for the rejection of a plaint if it appears to be barred by law.
    * **CPC, Order VII Rule 10:** This rule permits the return of a plaint if it lacks essential information or is defective.
    * **Act of 2015, Section 2(1)(c)(vii):** This section defines "commercial dispute" to include disputes arising from agreements relating to immovable property "used exclusively in trade or commerce".
    * **Act of 2015, Section 6:** This section confers jurisdiction on Commercial Courts to try commercial disputes of specified value.
    * **Act of 2015, Section 15:** This section provides for the transfer of commercial disputes to Commercial Courts.
* **Precedents Cited:**
    * **Kanchanganga Relators Pvt. Ltd. Vs. Monarch Infrastructure Developers (2019):** This case interpreted the meaning of "used" in the context of Section 2(1)(c)(vii) of the Act of 2015.
    * **Mansukhlal Dhanraj Jain and others Vs. Eknath Vithal Ogale (1995):** This case concerned the interpretation of the term "relating to" in a legal context.
    * **Jagmohan Behl Vs. State Bank of Indore (2017):** This case dealt with the application of the Act of 2015 to disputes concerning immovable property used for trade or commerce.
    * **Dineshkumar Gulabghand Agrawal v. Commissioner of Income-Tax (2004):** This case interpreted the meaning of "used" in the context of income tax law.
    * **Vasu Healthcare Private Vs. Gujarat Akruti TCG Biotech Limited (2017):** This case focused on the interpretation of "used" in the context of the Act of 2015.
    * **Ambalal Sarabhai Enterprises Limited Vs. K.S. Infraspace LLP (2020):** This case addressed the interpretation of "used" under the Act of 2015, clarifying that "used" means "actually used" or "being used".
* **Legal Doctrines:**
    * **Purposive Interpretation:** The court adopted a purposive interpretation of the Act of 2015, focusing on the legislative intent to expedite the resolution of high-value commercial disputes.
    * **Literal Interpretation:** The court adopted a literal interpretation of the word "used," emphasizing that it means "actually used" or "being used."

**4. Arguments Presented**

* **Applicants' Argument:**
    * The agreements and the land in question were intended for commercial development, indicating a commercial transaction.
    * The Kingranis intended to earn a profit by constructing and selling residential units, highlighting a commercial motive.
    * The dispute falls within the scope of Section 2(1)(c)(vii) of the Act of 2015, making it a commercial dispute.
    * The case of Kanchanganga Relators Pvt. Ltd. supports their claim, expanding the meaning of "used" to include "capable of being used".
* **Non-Applicants' Argument:**
    * The land was not "actually being used" for trade or commerce at the time of filing the suits.
    * The Act of 2015 requires the property to be "used" in trade or commerce, not merely intended for future use.
    * A liberal interpretation of the Act of 2015 would undermine the purpose of fast-tracking commercial disputes.
    * The decision in Vasu Healthcare Private Vs. Gujarat Akruti TCG Biotech Limited (2017) supports their position, interpreting "used" as "actually used" or "being used."

**5. Court's Analysis and Reasoning**

* **Key Findings:**
    * The court recognized that the Kingranis intended to use the land for commercial development by constructing a residential project and selling units.
    * The court acknowledged the legislative intent behind the Act of 2015 â€“ to ensure quick resolution of high-value commercial disputes.
    * The court emphasized the need for a strict interpretation of the word "used" as "actually used" or "being used" to avoid undermining the purpose of the Act.
* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The court interpreted Section 2(1)(c)(vii) of the Act of 2015 strictly, holding that the immovable property must be "actually used" or "being used" in trade or commerce to qualify as a commercial dispute.
    * The court rejected the view taken in Kanchanganga Relators Pvt. Ltd. that "capable of being used" or "intended to be used" qualifies as "used."
    * The court approved the interpretation of "used" as "actually used" or "being used" as established in Vasu Healthcare Private Vs. Gujarat Akruti TCG Biotech Limited (2017) and further affirmed by the Supreme Court in Ambalal Sarabhai Enterprises Limited Vs. K.S. Infraspace LLP (2020).
* **Application of Law:**
    * The court applied the strict interpretation of "used" to the facts of the case.
    * Since the land was not "actually used" or "being used" for trade or commerce at the time of filing the suits, the court found that the dispute did not qualify as a commercial dispute.
    * The court held that the trial court's decision to consider the dispute not a commercial one was correct.

**6. Judgment**

* **Final Decision:** The revision applications filed by J.P. Realties Pvt. Ltd. and J.P. Housing Pvt. Ltd. were dismissed.
* **Relief Granted:** No relief was granted to the applicants.
* **Orders:** The court upheld the trial court's decision.

**7. Implications**

* **Impact on Law:** This judgment reinforces the strict interpretation of the definition of "commercial dispute" under the Act of 2015. It clarifies that the property must be "actually used" or "being used" for trade or commerce for the dispute to be considered commercial.
* **Future Relevance:** This judgment provides guidance for future cases involving the definition of "commercial dispute" under the Act of 2015. It serves as a precedent for courts to apply a strict interpretation of the "used" requirement.
* **Broader Context:** The decision emphasizes the importance of balancing the objectives of efficient dispute resolution with the need for a clear and predictable legal framework. It highlights the need to ensure that only genuine commercial disputes are channeled to the specialized Commercial Courts, maximizing their effectiveness.

**8. Summary Points**

* Disputes concerning immovable property are not automatically commercial disputes. 
* The immovable property must be "actually used" or "being used" in trade or commerce for the dispute to be considered commercial under the Act of 2015.
* The court rejected a broad interpretation of "used" to include "capable of being used" or "intended to be used" for trade or commerce.
* The judgment reiterates the importance of a strict interpretation of the Act of 2015 to ensure the effective operation of Commercial Courts.
