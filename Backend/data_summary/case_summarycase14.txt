* **Case Name:** Axis Bank Limited vs Mira Gehani And 8 Ors

## 2. Background and Context

* **Brief Overview:** This case involved multiple Commercial Suits filed before the Commercial Division of the Bombay High Court. The central issue was the interpretation of an amendment to Order VIII of the Code of Civil Procedure (CPC) introduced by the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 ("Commercial Courts Act"). This amendment set a strict 120-day deadline for Defendants to file their written statements in Commercial Suits. Several Defendants failed to meet this deadline and filed Notices of Motion seeking condonation of delay.
* **Key Issues:** 
    1. Whether, in light of the Commercial Courts Act amendments, Defendants could file their written statements after the 120-day deadline in a Commercial Suit.
    2. How to reconcile the Commercial Courts Act with the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules, 1980, regarding the Court's power to condone delay.
    3. The interpretation and application of Order VIII Rules 9 and 10 of the CPC in the context of the Commercial Courts Act.

* **Legal Doctrines:**
    * **Harmonious Construction:**  The court emphasizes the need to interpret statutes in a way that gives effect to all provisions, resolving conflicts by finding a common ground.
    * **Special vs. General Provisions:**  When specific provisions in a special act conflict with general provisions in another act, the specific provisions prevail.
    * **Mandatory vs. Directory Provisions:**  The court determines whether a provision is mandatory, requiring strict compliance, or directory, providing a guideline that can be flexibly applied.
    * **Non Obstante Clause:**  The court examines the effect of "notwithstanding anything contained in this Act" clauses, which indicate the legislature's intention for a provision to override other conflicting laws.
    * **Stare Decisis:**  The court refers to precedents, emphasizing the importance of adhering to past decisions and the impact on legal certainty.
    * **Res Judicata:** The court considers the principle of res judicata, which prevents the re-litigation of issues already decided by a court.
    * **Inherent Powers of the Court:**  The court examines the court's inherent power under Section 151 of the CPC, which allows it to make orders necessary for the ends of justice or to prevent abuse of the process of the court.
    * **Mischief Rule:** The court applies the mischief rule of statutory interpretation, which considers the common law before the act, the defect the act was meant to address, the remedy provided by the act, and the true reason for the remedy.
    * **Dura Lex Sed Lex:**  The court acknowledges the principle that the law may be harsh, but it is still the law, emphasizing that equitable considerations cannot override clear statutory provisions.

## 4. Arguments Presented

**A. Applicants/Defendants' Arguments:**

* **Condonation of Delay:**  Defendants argued that the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules, 1980, empowered the Court to condone the delay in filing their written statements, even beyond the 120-day limit set by the Commercial Courts Act.
* **Overriding of CPC:**  They contended that the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules prevailed over the CPC, as amended by the Commercial Courts Act, citing the Apex Court's decision in Iridium.
* **Section 16(3) of the Commercial Courts Act:**  Defendants argued that this provision did not apply to the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules because it referred to "Rule" (singular), which they claimed did not include the Rules made under Section 129 of the CPC.
* **Section 21 of the Commercial Courts Act:**  They argued that the "save as otherwise provided" language in this section indicated that the Act would yield to other existing provisions, including the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules.
* **Order VIII Rules 9 and 10 of the CPC:**  They asserted that the Court retained the discretion to take on record belated written statements under these rules, even after the 120-day deadline had passed.
* **Equity and Emergent Circumstances:**  They argued that the Court, as a court of equity, should consider emergent circumstances that prevented Defendants from filing their written statements on time.

**B. Plaintiffs' Arguments:**

* **Legislative Intent:**  Plaintiffs emphasized that the Commercial Courts Act aimed to expedite the disposal of Commercial Suits and that the 120-day deadline was mandatory.
* **Section 16 of the Commercial Courts Act:** Plaintiffs argued that Section 16(2) specifically mandated Commercial Divisions to follow the provisions of the CPC as amended by the Commercial Courts Act, including the 120-day deadline.
* **Section 21 of the Commercial Courts Act:**  Plaintiffs contended that the non-obstante clause in this section clearly indicated the legislature's intention for the Act to override any conflicting laws, including the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules.
* **Iridium Distinguishable:** Plaintiffs distinguished the Iridium decision because it involved a general amendment to the CPC, while the Commercial Courts Act was a special act.
* **Order VIII Rules 9 and 10:**  Plaintiffs argued that applying these rules to extend the deadline beyond 120 days would render the 120-day deadline meaningless and contradict the purpose of the Commercial Courts Act.
* **Equal Treatment:**  Plaintiffs argued that applying the 120-day deadline inconsistently across different courts would create unequal treatment for litigants.
* **Non-disclosure of Documents:** Plaintiffs argued that failing to disclose all documents in their power, possession, control, or custody did not constitute a "bar in law" under Order VII, Rule 11(d) of the CPC.

* **Key Findings:**
    * The Commercial Courts Act aimed to expedite the disposal of Commercial Suits and promote investor confidence.
    * The amendments to Order V Rule 1, Order VIII Rules 1 and 10 of the CPC introduced by the Commercial Courts Act were mandatory in nature.
    * The Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules, 1980, were subject to the Commercial Courts Act, as the latter was a special act enacted later.
    * The phrase "Rule of the jurisdictional High Court" in Section 16(3) of the Commercial Courts Act included the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules.
    * Order VIII Rules 9 and 10 could not be used to circumvent the 120-day deadline for filing written statements in Commercial Suits.
    * The court was bound to apply the law as laid down by the legislature, even if it might lead to harsh consequences in some cases.
* **Interpretation of Law:**
    * The court interpreted the language of the Commercial Courts Act, specifically Sections 16 and 21, as clear and unambiguous, demonstrating the legislature's intent to override any conflicting provisions.
    * The court interpreted the term "Rule" in Section 16(3) broadly, including rules made under Section 129 of the CPC, which governs the Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules.
    * The court emphasized the mandatory nature of the 120-day deadline, noting that the legislation provided for consequences (forfeiture of right to file a written statement) and explicitly removed the court's discretion to take on record a belated statement.
* **Application of Law:**
    * The court applied the principle of harmonious construction to resolve conflicts between different provisions of the CPC and the Commercial Courts Act, giving precedence to the specific provisions of the later special act.
    * The court applied the legal doctrines of mandatory vs. directory provisions, special vs. general provisions, and non-obstante clauses to determine the relative weight and application of different laws.
    * The court applied the maxim dura lex sed lex to reject the defendants' arguments based on equity and emergent circumstances, upholding the supremacy of the law even when it might cause hardship.

## 6. Judgment

* **Final Decision:** The court decided in favor of the Plaintiffs, upholding the mandatory nature of the 120-day deadline for filing written statements in Commercial Suits.
* **Relief Granted:** No specific relief or damages were awarded in this case, as it was a question of law rather than a dispute over specific facts.
* **Orders:**
    * The court ordered that, in Commercial Suits, Defendants could not file written statements after the expiry of 120 days from the date of service of the Writ of Summons.
    * The court recommended that the Bombay High Court revise its Original Side Rules to align them with the Commercial Courts Act and ensure the expeditious disposal of Commercial Suits.
    * The court also recommended the introduction of strict time limits for Plaintiffs to remove office objections and serve the Writ of Summons, to promote efficiency in handling Commercial Suits.

## 8. Summary Points

* The Commercial Courts Act, 2015, introduced a mandatory 120-day deadline for Defendants to file written statements in Commercial Suits.
* The Bombay High Court (Original Side) Rules, 1980, were subject to the later-enacted Commercial Courts Act.
* The court's discretion to condone delay in filing written statements was removed by the Commercial Courts Act, rendering Order VIII Rules 9 and 10 inapplicable in this context.
* The court emphasized the need for uniformity in applying the provisions of the Commercial Courts Act across all courts.
* The court recommended the implementation of strict timelines for both Plaintiffs and Defendants in Commercial Suits to promote efficiency and expeditious disposal.
